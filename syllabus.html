The Big Picture

BIO 004 is a study of the structural organization of the human body, from cellular to organismal level. Throughout the course, you will work with microscopic slides, prosected cadaver material, anatomical models, and clinical case scenarios.

By the end of the term, you will be able to:

  • Identify and distinguish tissues based on currently recognized criteria.
  • Describe the basic microscopic and gross anatomy of the major systems of the human body.
  • Identify, in the laboratory, major microscopic and gross structures of the major systems of the human body.

How This Course Works

Every week is built around one clinical case. You will not be told what the case is. You get a patient presentation, and your team works up the differential diagnosis using the anatomy you are studying that week.

Class meets Monday through Thursday. Lecture runs from 10:00 to 11:20 AM, followed by lab from 11:30 AM to 2:35 PM. Each day has a specific role in the case arc, and each day has its own pre-work that you complete the night before.

What each day looks like

Monday: Case intro. You meet the patient. In lecture we work through their presentation, history, and the anatomy that drives their complaint. Your team forms an initial differential based on Sunday night's pre-work and the case details. Lab begins work with the structures that matter for this case.

Tuesday: Case prep. Your team consolidates Monday's anatomy and adds Tuesday's new material from last night's pre-work. In lecture we expand into related structures. In lab you go deeper with models, slides, and prosected specimens. Your differential gets refined as new anatomy comes in.

Wednesday: Case run. Your team works through the week's anatomy applied to the case. You tackle the application questions, rule differentials in and out, and build the reasoning you will defend on Thursday.

Thursday: TBL and case presentation. The culminating day. You start with the individual readiness test (IRAT), then the team version (TRAT). From there, each team presents its differential and reasoning. I facilitate a class discussion where teams engage with each other's cases and defend their thinking, and we uncover what the case actually was together.

Upper and lower extremity muscles are covered primarily during lab sessions rather than as dedicated lecture days.

Pre-work is not optional. Your readiness Monday through Thursday depends on the reading, videos, and study guides you complete the night before. Your individual TBL scores depend on it. Show up prepared, and you will thrive in this course.

The Unknown Cases

There are eight weekly cases, and you will not be told the diagnosis in advance. Each case begins with a patient presentation. Your team works through the anatomy of the week, builds a differential, rules alternatives in or out, and presents your reasoning in Thursday's TBL.

This is a heavily memorization-driven course. Do not read the case-based framing as permission to skip the memorization work. You will be learning the anatomy of every major body system in eight weeks, and that knowledge has to be in your head. The cases give your memorization a clinical purpose. You memorize the anatomy so you can reason about the patient in front of you, not the other way around.

This is how clinicians think. Memorize the structures cold, then use them to reason from presentation to probable cause, the same way you will reason about a real patient.

Some cases will resolve cleanly. Some will have misleading features that pull you toward the wrong diagnosis. That is the point. Your differential, your reasoning, and your ability to defend your answer matter as much as landing on the right name.

Team-Based Learning

You will be placed on a team during week one. That team stays together for all eight weeks. You will learn with them, struggle with them, and present the weekly case together. In my years of teaching this way, students who engage with their team earn grades two to three times better than students who try to work alone.

Your TBL score each Thursday comes from two quizzes plus team discussion.

The two quizzes

  • IRAT (Individual Readiness Assessment Test): 10 to 15 questions on the week's anatomy, taken solo at the start of Thursday. Weighted 3x, because individual preparation is what drives your performance in this course.
  • TRAT (Team Readiness Assessment Test): Same quiz, taken as a team. Weighted 1x. Most teams score 90% or higher here, so your IRAT is what separates one TBL grade from another.

Application questions

During Wednesday's case run, your team works through clinical application questions based on the week's case. These are not scored for points, but you are expected to explain your team's reasoning in Thursday's presentation, so every member needs to be able to defend your answers.

Team presentations and class discussion

After IRAT and TRAT, each team presents its differential and reasoning to the class. I facilitate the discussion, and teams engage with each other's cases. You may be called on to explain your team's thinking or respond to another team's reasoning. This is how we move from memorized anatomy to real clinical reasoning.

Scoring: 3x IRAT + 1x TRAT. Example: IRAT 7/10, TRAT 9/10 gives (7 × 3) + 9 = 30/40 (75%). The heavy IRAT weighting pushes you to show up prepared.

There are no TBL makeups. If you miss a Thursday, you earn a zero for that TBL. Your lowest weekly TBL score is dropped at the end of the term.

Lab Exams and Homework Bundles

There are five lab practical exams across the term. Each exam covers the module or modules that came before it. You will rotate through timed stations and identify structures on slides, photographs, anatomical models, and prosected human tissue. Spelling counts. Bring a pen.

Before each exam, you will hand in a completed homework bundle. This bundle contains case prep worksheets and metacognition reflections built up during the module. You submit your bundle at the door before the exam starts, completed. If you walk in without a finished bundle, you walk back out.

Doors lock when the exam begins. If you are late, you will not be able to enter the classroom. Plan your commute, your coffee, and your parking accordingly.

In the Classroom

The rules for devices differ between lecture and lab. Both are firm in their own way.

Lecture: devices required, for course work only

You will use your laptop or tablet in lecture for Team-Based Learning activities, IRATs, TRATs, and in-class exercises delivered through InteDashboard. Bring your device charged. You will also install a lockdown browser (I will specify which one during the first week) for secure assessments.

Your device is for course activities only. Personal browsing, social media, messaging, streaming, and unrelated work are not permitted during lecture. Phones stay in your bag unless I tell you otherwise. If your screen shows anything unrelated to the day's work, that is a violation of this policy.

Lab: no personal devices at all

Lab runs on a stricter rule. When you walk in, take out only what you need for that session: lab materials, notes, and pens. Place everything on the table in front of you. Everything else, including your phone, smartwatch, laptop, and tablet, stays in your bag. Your bag goes in the assigned cubby.

This mirrors clinical norms. You will not have your phone out at the bedside, in the OR, or during a nursing skills exam. We practice that professional standard here.

If you need to step out

If a situation arises where you genuinely need to check your phone during class time, you take your entire bag outside, not just the device. Use your phone in the hallway or outside the building, then return with everything secured in your bag. The bag leaves together. The bag returns together. This keeps the policy clean and equal for everyone.

No photos in lab, ever

The cadaveric specimens you are privileged to study are people who donated their bodies to your education. Photographing them, even for your own study purposes, violates their donation agreement and California law. The no-devices policy in lab protects you, the program, and the donors.

Discipline policy

Device violations are handled seriously. Violations include using your device for anything other than course work during lecture, and bringing any personal device out of your bag during lab.
  • First violation: You are removed from class for the day and receive zero points for any assignment, quiz, or assessment scheduled for that day.
  • Second violation: You meet with the Dean. You are suspended from the course for three days. Anything you miss during that suspension is on you and cannot be made up. A meeting with the Dean is required before you return to class.
  • Third violation: You are dropped from the course. At three violations you will have missed three classes, which triggers automatic removal under the attendance policy.

No warnings. The policy is posted here and will be stated on the first day so you can plan accordingly. If you have a documented emergency or accommodation that requires a device exception, come talk to me before class so we can work out an appropriate arrangement.

Thrive in This Course

Anatomy is a challenging course. There is no sugar-coating that. But it is also one of the most rewarding, and the study skills you build here will carry into every program that comes after.

This is not a passive class.

You will not pass by watching lectures and re-reading chapters. Successful students build active learning tools: color-coded diagrams, concept maps, flashcards, self-quizzes, and study sheets that compare structures side by side.

Flip your prep-to-recall ratio.

Most struggling students spend 90% of their study time preparing (retyping notes, reorganizing folders, rewatching lectures) and 10% actually testing themselves. Flip that. Aim for 20% prep, 80% active recall. Quantity matters, but quality matters more.

Your Crew is your secret weapon.

You will be placed on a team at the start of the term, and your team stays together for all eight weeks. Trade contact info in week one. Establish a group chat. Show up for each other. In my years of teaching this way, students who work with their team earn grades 2 to 3 times better than students who work alone.

Your grade is feedback, not a verdict. It is one data point in your journey, not the sum of who you are. If you fall short of your expectations, do not give up. Adjust your approach, ask for help, and try again. You belong here.
