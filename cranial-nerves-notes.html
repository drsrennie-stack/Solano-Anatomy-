Cranial Nerves

Twelve paired nerves emerging from the brain and brain stem. Their functional categories, foramina of exit, individual roles, bedside testing, and the classic pathology patterns for each.

Twelve pairs of cranial nerves leave the brain and brain stem, exit the skull through specific foramina, and reach targets in the head, neck, and beyond. Before learning each nerve, anchor three frameworks: the brain stem map, the seven functional information categories, and the foramina table.

Where the CNs Come From

Brain Stem in Three Parts

Most cranial nerves emerge from or pass through the brain stem.

The Three Major Parts

  • Midbrain: origin of CN III and CN IV
  • Pons (basilar pons): origin of CN V, VI, VII, and parts of VIII
  • Medulla: origin of CN IX, X, XI, XII, and parts of VIII

CN I (olfactory) attaches directly to the cerebrum. CN II (optic) attaches to the diencephalon. The other ten emerge from the brain stem.

Reminder: in the CNS, clusters of cell bodies are called nuclei and bundles of axons are called tracts. The cranial nerve nuclei live inside the brain stem; the nerves themselves are PNS structures.

Functional Information Categories

Seven Categories the CNs Carry

Cranial nerves convey one or more types of information. The categories use four-letter shorthand: G (general) or S (special), then S (somatic) or V (visceral), then A (afferent/sensory) or E (efferent/motor).

Three cranial nerves are purely sensory: olfactory (smell), optic (vision), and vestibulocochlear (hearing and balance). All three carry Special Somatic Afferent information and each has a defining bedside test.

CN I

Olfactory Nerve

  • General function: olfaction (smell)
  • Exits the skull: cribriform plate of the ethmoid bone, via the olfactory foramina
  • Information: Special Somatic Afferent (olfaction)

Course

Olfactory stimuli are picked up by specialized chemoreceptors on bipolar primary sensory neurons in the olfactory neuroepithelium of the upper nasal cavity. Their axons pass through the cribriform plate to synapse in the olfactory bulb.

Testing CN I

  • Patient closes their eyes and occludes one nostril
  • Present a familiar non-irritating odor (coffee, cinnamon, soap)
  • Test each side separately

Pathology: Anosmia

Definition: loss of olfactory function.

Unilateral anosmia: usually not detected by the patient because the contralateral side compensates.

Bilateral anosmia: noticeable; presents with decreased taste because smell contributes heavily to flavor perception.

Causes:

  • Head trauma (tearing of bipolar neurons at the cribriform plate)
  • Viral infections (damage to olfactory neuroepithelium)
  • Obstruction of nasal passages
  • Intracranial lesions: tumors, granulomas, abscesses
CN II

Optic Nerve

  • General function: vision
  • Exits the skull: optic canal (in the orbit)
  • Information: Special Somatic Afferent (vision)

Course

Retinal ganglion cell axons (technically CNS, so really tracts rather than peripheral nerves) form the optic nerve.

Retina → Optic nerve → Optic chiasm → Optic tract

Testing CN II

  • Visual acuity (Snellen chart)
  • Ophthalmoscopic exam: visualize the retina, retinal blood vessels, and optic disc; look for papilledema
  • Visual fields (confrontation testing)

Pathology: Anopia & Papilledema

Anopia: visual field defect. Hemianopia means partial loss of the visual field.

Lesion location maps to defect:

  • Optic nerve (in front of the chiasm): defect in one eye only
  • Optic tract (behind the chiasm): similar deficits in both eyes
  • Optic chiasm: loss of peripheral visual fields in both eyes (bitemporal hemianopia, classic for pituitary tumor)

Papilledema: swelling of the optic disc. Cause: increased intracranial pressure (meningitis, tumor, hydrocephalus).

CN VIII

Vestibulocochlear Nerve

  • General function: hearing and equilibrium
  • Exits the skull: internal auditory canal
  • Information: Special Somatic Afferent (hearing and vestibular sensation)

Two Components

  • Cochlear branch: hearing
  • Vestibular branch: balance and head position

Testing CN VIII

  • Tuning fork tests (Rinne, Weber) to detect hearing loss and distinguish its type
Hearing Loss Types

Three Patterns

  • Conductive hearing loss: a problem with the transmission of sound through the outer or middle ear (cerumen, otitis media, ossicle dysfunction)
  • Sensorineural hearing loss: a problem with the cochlea or the cochlear nerve itself (presbycusis, noise damage, acoustic neuroma)
  • Mixed: features of both
Clinical logic: tuning fork tests separate "where is the problem?" If sound conducts better through bone than air on the affected side, the issue is conductive. If air conduction is preserved but sound is faint everywhere on that side, the issue is sensorineural.

Three nerves move the eyeball: oculomotor, trochlear, and abducens. Oculomotor does most of the work; trochlear and abducens each control a single muscle. They are tested together using the H-pattern, which stresses each muscle in isolation.

CN III

Oculomotor Nerve

  • General function: movement of the eye; constriction of the pupil
  • Exits the skull: superior orbital fissure
  • Information: GSE to extraocular muscles; GVE (parasympathetic) to pupillary constrictor and ciliary muscles

GSE: Skeletal Muscles of the Eye

  • Levator palpebrae superioris (eyelid elevation)
  • Superior rectus
  • Inferior rectus
  • Medial rectus
  • Inferior oblique

GVE: Parasympathetic

  • Pupillary constrictor muscle (pupil constriction)
  • Ciliary muscle (lens accommodation for near vision)
CN IV

Trochlear Nerve

  • General function: movement of the eye
  • Exits the skull: superior orbital fissure
  • Information: GSE to a single muscle

The One Muscle

  • Superior oblique: causes depression and intorsion (medial rotation) of the eye, especially when the eye is adducted
Two distinctions of CN IV: it is the only cranial nerve that exits from the dorsal aspect of the brain stem, and the only cranial nerve whose axons cross before innervating their target.
CN VI

Abducens Nerve

  • General function: movement of the eye
  • Exits the skull: superior orbital fissure
  • Information: GSE to a single muscle

The One Muscle

  • Lateral rectus: abduction of the eye (turns the eye outward, away from the nose)
Memory cue: the name abducens is the function. The nerve abducts the eye.
Combined Testing

The H-Pattern Exam

All extraocular skeletal muscles are tested together. The patient follows a moving target (finger or pen) traced in an H pattern, stopping at each cardinal direction.

What the H-Pattern Reveals

  • Failure to move the eye in a specific direction points to dysfunction in the muscle responsible for that direction, and therefore the nerve supplying it
  • Cannot abduct (eye drifts medially): CN VI (lateral rectus) lesion
  • Cannot look down and in: CN IV (superior oblique) lesion
  • Most other directions impaired, plus ptosis and dilated pupil: CN III (oculomotor) lesion
Eye Movement Pathology

Nystagmus

Definition: repetitive, uncontrolled eye movements that impact vision and depth perception. Can lead to problems with balance and coordination.

Types

  • Horizontal nystagmus: side-to-side movement
  • Vertical nystagmus: up-and-down movement
  • Rotary (torsional) nystagmus: circular movement

Origin

  • Can be congenital or acquired
  • Acquired forms can result from lesions in the brain or in the inner ear
  • Drugs and toxins (including alcohol) commonly cause horizontal nystagmus
Pupillary Light Reflex

The Two-Nerve Reflex

Shining light in one eye causes both pupils to constrict. The reflex tests two cranial nerves at once.

  • Sensory (afferent) limb: CN II (optic nerve) carries the light signal in
  • Motor (efferent) limb: CN III (oculomotor) carries the parasympathetic constriction signal out

Interpreting Findings

  • No constriction in either eye when light shines in the affected eye: afferent (CN II) problem in that eye
  • Constriction of the contralateral eye but not the affected eye when light shines in either eye: efferent (CN III) problem in the affected eye

The trigeminal and facial nerves between them serve almost everything you do with your face. Trigeminal handles sensation across the entire face plus the muscles that chew. Facial handles the muscles that emote, plus taste from the front of the tongue and three of the four salivary glands.

CN V

Trigeminal Nerve

  • General function: sensation to face, mouth, sinuses, meninges; motor to muscles of mastication and tensor tympani
  • Exits the skull: three divisions, three foramina (Single Room Occupancy)
    • V1 Ophthalmic: Superior orbital fissure
    • V2 Maxillary: Foramen Rotundum
    • V3 Mandibular: Foramen Ovale
  • Information: GSA (sensory) and SVE (motor)
CN V Sensory

GSA: Sensation to the Face

Each of the three divisions covers a horizontal band of the face.

Territory

  • V1 Ophthalmic: forehead, upper eyelid, cornea, dorsal nose
  • V2 Maxillary: mid-face, upper lip, upper teeth, lower eyelid
  • V3 Mandibular: lower jaw, lower lip, lower teeth, anterior two-thirds of the tongue (general sensation only)

Modalities Carried

  • Touch, pain, temperature
  • Joint position sense, vibration
  • From: face, mouth, anterior two-thirds of tongue, nasal sinuses, meninges
CN V Motor

SVE: Muscles of Mastication

The motor root travels with V3 (mandibular division).

Targets

  • Muscles of mastication: masseter, temporalis, medial and lateral pterygoids, mylohyoid, anterior belly of digastric
  • Tensor tympani (middle ear): dampens loud sounds
CN V Testing

Examination

  • Sensory exam of the face (light touch and pain) in each of the three divisions, bilaterally
  • Compare sharp vs soft stimuli, looking for symmetry side to side
  • Corneal reflex: touching the cornea elicits a blink (sensory limb is V1; motor limb is CN VII)
  • Have patient bite down; palpate the masseter and temporalis for symmetric contraction

Pathology: Trigeminal Neuralgia (Tic Douloureux)

Definition: recurrent episodes of brief, severe pain lasting from seconds to a few minutes.

Location: most often in V2 or V3 distribution.

Demographics: typically appears after age 35.

Triggers: chewing, shaving, brushing teeth, or even lightly touching a specific area of the face.

Causes: mostly unknown. Occasionally caused by compression of the trigeminal nerve by an aberrant blood vessel. Tumor must be ruled out. Also occurs in multiple sclerosis (demyelination).

CN VII

Facial Nerve

  • General function: muscles of facial expression; taste; lacrimation; salivation
  • Exits the skull: internal auditory canal; exits the skull at the stylomastoid foramen
  • Information: SVE (motor); GVE (parasympathetic); SVA (taste); GSA (small region near external auditory meatus)
CN VII Functions in Detail

Four Roles

SVE: Motor

  • Muscles of facial expression
  • Stapedius (middle ear): dampens loud sounds

GVE: Parasympathetic

  • Lacrimal gland (tear production)
  • Submandibular and sublingual salivary glands
  • Nasal and palatine glands

SVA: Taste

  • Anterior two-thirds of the tongue

GSA: Sensation

  • Small region near the external auditory meatus
CN VII Testing

Examination

  • Have the patient make faces: smile, frown, pucker, blink, puff cheeks, wrinkle forehead, raise eyebrows
  • Look for symmetry on both sides
  • Apply taste stimulus to both sides of the anterior tongue

Pathology: Bell's Palsy vs Stroke

The classic teaching question with CN VII is how to distinguish a peripheral lesion (Bell's palsy) from a central lesion (stroke). The answer comes from the cortical wiring: the upper face receives motor input from both motor cortices, while the lower face receives input from only the contralateral cortex.

Bell's palsy (peripheral CN VII lesion): entire side of the face is affected. The patient cannot wrinkle the forehead or close the eye on the affected side.

Stroke (central upper motor neuron lesion): only the lower face is affected on the contralateral side. The patient can still wrinkle the forehead and close both eyes because the upper face receives bilateral cortical input.

Bell's palsy specifics: idiopathic in most cases, often viral. Usually unilateral. Most patients recover fully within weeks to months.

Three cranial nerves share the jugular foramen: glossopharyngeal, vagus, and accessory. Together they handle swallowing, voice, gag reflex, parasympathetic supply to most of the body, and the sternocleidomastoid and trapezius muscles. The gag reflex elegantly tests two of them at once.

CN IX

Glossopharyngeal Nerve

  • General function: pharyngeal muscles; carotid body reflexes; salivation
  • Exits the skull: jugular foramen

Information Carried

  • SVE: stylopharyngeus muscle
  • GVE (parasympathetic): parotid gland (salivation)
  • SVA (taste): posterior one-third of the tongue
  • GVA: chemoreceptors and baroreceptors of the carotid body
  • GSA: middle ear, region near the external auditory meatus, pharynx, posterior one-third of the tongue (general sensation)
CN X

Vagus Nerve

  • General function: parasympathetic to most thoracoabdominal organs; laryngeal muscles (voice); pharyngeal muscles; aortic arch reflexes
  • Exits the skull: jugular foramen

Information Carried

  • SVE: pharyngeal muscles (swallowing) and laryngeal muscles (voice)
  • GVE (parasympathetic): heart, lungs, digestive tract (down to the splenic flexure of the colon)
  • SVA (taste): epiglottis and pharynx
  • GVA: chemoreceptors and baroreceptors of the aortic arch
  • GSA: pharynx, meninges, small region near the external auditory meatus
About the name: "vagus" means "wandering" in Latin. CN X earns the name by traveling far from the head down through the thorax and into the abdomen, supplying parasympathetic input to most internal organs along the way.
Combined Testing

Why CN IX and CN X Are Tested Together

Glossopharyngeal and vagus share the pharynx as a target. Many bedside tests can therefore evaluate both at once.

Examination

  • Voice quality: ask the patient to speak; listen for hoarseness (CN X laryngeal muscles)
  • Soft palate elevation: have patient say "Ahhhh"; the soft palate should rise symmetrically and the uvula should remain midline
  • Gag reflex: tested bilaterally
  • Swallowing: ask the patient to swallow; observe smoothness and listen for cough
  • Bitter taste: applied to the posterior one-third of the tongue (CN IX taste)

The Gag Reflex: A Two-Nerve Test

Method: use a long-handled swab to touch the pharyngeal wall on each side.

Reflex arc:

  • Sensory (afferent) limb: CN IX (glossopharyngeal)
  • Motor (efferent) limb: CN X (vagus)

Findings and what they mean:

  • Normal: symmetrical elevation of the uvula, fauces, and tonsillar arches
  • Non-symmetrical elevation when one side is touched: problem with the sensory arc (CN IX) on that side
  • No movement of the uvula at all with the gag reflex: problem with the motor arc (CN X), i.e. muscle paralysis
CN XI

Accessory Nerve (Spinal Accessory)

  • General function: head turning and shoulder shrugging
  • Exits the skull: jugular foramen (enters the cranium through the foramen magnum)
  • Information: SVE to sternocleidomastoid and upper trapezius
The unusual journey: CN XI is the only cranial nerve whose fibers originate from the cervical spinal cord (C1 to C5/C6), travel up through the foramen magnum into the cranium, then exit again through the jugular foramen to reach their muscle targets in the neck.
CN XI Testing

Examination

  • SCM test: turn the patient's head to one side, then the other, against resistance. The contralateral SCM contracts to turn the head.
  • Trapezius test: have the patient shrug shoulders upward against resistance
  • Observe the SCM and trapezius for atrophy or fasciculations
Quick logic: turning the head to the left requires contraction of the right SCM (which pulls the mastoid process toward the sternum). So weak head-turning to the left points to right CN XI dysfunction.

The hypoglossal nerve does one job: move the tongue. The classic clinical pearl is which way the tongue deviates with a lesion. Use this final tab to lock in CN XII and to consolidate the entire 12-nerve set with a master reference table and the canonical study anchors.

CN XII

Hypoglossal Nerve

  • General function: tongue movement
  • Exits the skull: hypoglossal canal
  • Information: GSE (somatic motor) to the intrinsic muscles of the tongue
CN XII Testing

Examination

  • Observe the tongue at rest for fasciculations or wasting
  • Have the patient protrude the tongue; look for deviation
  • Test strength on both sides (push tongue against the cheek; examiner resists from the outside)

Pathology: The Tongue Deviates Toward the Lesion

Rule: with a hypoglossal nerve lesion, the tongue deviates toward the side of the lesion when protruded.

Why: the genioglossus muscle on each side pulls the tongue forward and toward the opposite side. When the right hypoglossal nerve is intact, the right genioglossus pulls the tongue tip forward and to the left. When the right hypoglossal nerve is intact and the left is damaged, only the right genioglossus works, pulling the tongue forward and to the left (away from the working nerve, which means toward the lesion).

Example: if the left hypoglossal nerve is lesioned, the tongue deviates toward the left on protrusion (because the working right genioglossus pulls the tongue forward and to the left).
