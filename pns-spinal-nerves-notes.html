PNS: Spinal Nerves & Peripheral Nervous System

Spinal nerve architecture, the four major plexuses and the nerves they spin off, intercostal nerves, dermatomes, and the classic peripheral nerve injury patterns.

The peripheral nervous system is everything outside the brain and cord: the receptors that pick up the world, the nerves that carry signals, the ganglia where cell bodies cluster, and the motor endings that move us. Before tracing individual nerves, get the framework.

Definition

What the PNS Is

The PNS includes all neural structures outside the brain and spinal cord.

Components

  • Sensory (afferent) receptors
  • Peripheral nerves and their associated structures
  • Ganglia (clusters of cell bodies in the PNS)
  • Motor (efferent) endings

Function

  • Carries input from sensory receptors to the CNS
  • Carries motor output from the CNS to effectors
  • Allows movement, response, and self-care
CNS vs. PNS Vocabulary

Terminology Crosswalk

The same structures use different names depending on whether they live in the CNS or PNS.

Cell BodiesAxons
CNSNucleiTracts
PNSGangliaNerves
Memory cue: in the CNS, cells form nuclei and axons form tracts. In the PNS, cells form ganglia and axons form nerves. Same biology, different addresses.
Sensory Side

Sensory Receptors: Function

Receptors respond to changes (stimuli) in the internal or external environment.

General Sequence

  1. Stimulus activates a sensory receptor
  2. Receptor triggers a nerve impulse along an afferent (sensory) PNS axon
  3. Information travels to the brain
  4. Brain produces sensation (awareness) and perception (interpretation)
Example: a pebble in your shoe. The sensation is localized deep pressure. The perception is "there is a pebble in my shoe."
Receptor Classification

How Receptors Are Sorted

Receptors are classified three ways: by location, by stimulus type, and by structure.

By Location

  • Exteroceptors: respond to stimuli at body surface
  • Interoceptors (visceroceptors): respond to stimuli inside body, in viscera
  • Proprioceptors: respond to position and movement of body parts

By Stimulus Type

  • Mechanoreceptors: mechanical force, including touch, pressure (baroreceptors), vibration, stretch
  • Thermoreceptors: temperature
  • Chemoreceptors: chemicals, taste, smell
  • Photoreceptors: light
  • Nociceptors: pain (tissue damage)

By Structure

  • Encapsulated: wrapped in connective tissue capsule
  • Nonencapsulated (free nerve endings): bare dendrites
Nerve Structure

What a Nerve Is

A nerve is a cordlike organ of the PNS, made of parallel bundles of peripheral axons (some myelinated, some not), supported by connective tissue.

Two Classifications

  • Cranial nerves: arise from the brain or brain stem (12 pairs, covered separately)
  • Spinal nerves: arise from the spinal cord (31 pairs)
PNS Functional Divisions

Big Picture Map

The PNS is functionally divided into the somatic system (voluntary, skeletal muscle) and the autonomic system (involuntary, viscera). The autonomic system splits further into sympathetic, parasympathetic, and enteric divisions.

PNS → Somatic (voluntary)
PNS → Autonomic → Sympathetic, Parasympathetic, Enteric

The autonomic divisions get their own dedicated note set. This file focuses on the somatic side: spinal nerves and the named peripheral nerves they form.

Every spinal nerve is a mixed nerve. Sensory axons enter through the back, motor axons leave through the front, and they merge just outside the cord into a single nerve trunk. The connective tissue wrapping is the same idea as muscle wrapping, just with different names.

Roots and Rootlets

How a Spinal Nerve Connects to the Cord

Roots are bundles of axons that connect each spinal nerve to a segment of the cord. Rootlets are the even smaller bundles that converge to form a root.

Posterior (Dorsal) Root and Rootlets

  • Contain only sensory axons
  • Carry impulses from receptors (skin, muscles, internal organs) toward the CNS
  • Each posterior root has a posterior (dorsal) root ganglion, a swelling that contains the cell bodies of sensory neurons

Anterior (Ventral) Root and Rootlets

  • Contain only motor axons
  • Carry impulses from the CNS to effectors (skeletal muscles and glands)
Quick rule: sensory in the back (dorsal), motor in the front (ventral). Sensory and motor merge just before exiting the intervertebral foramen, which is why every spinal nerve is mixed.
Connective Tissue Wrappings

Coverings of a Spinal Nerve

Three layers of connective tissue organize the axons of a peripheral nerve, parallel to the wrapping pattern in skeletal muscle.

Layers (innermost to outermost)

  • Endoneurium: innermost layer, surrounds individual axons (myelinated and unmyelinated)
  • Fascicle: a group of axons bundled together with their endoneurium
  • Perineurium: middle layer, thicker, wraps each fascicle
  • Epineurium: outermost layer, covers the entire nerve and is continuous with the dura mater
Axon → Endoneurium → Fascicle → Perineurium → Epineurium → Whole Nerve
Branching After Exit

Distribution: The Rami

After passing through the intervertebral foramen, a spinal nerve divides into branches called rami. Each ramus carries both sensory and motor fibers to its target territory.

Posterior (Dorsal) Ramus

  • Serves deep muscles and skin of the posterior trunk

Anterior (Ventral) Ramus

  • Serves muscles and structures of the upper and lower limbs
  • Serves the skin of the lateral and anterior trunk
  • Most anterior rami contribute to plexuses (network arrangements)

Meningeal Branch

  • Re-enters the vertebral cavity through the intervertebral foramen
  • Supplies vertebrae, vertebral ligaments, blood vessels of the spinal cord, and meninges

Rami Communicantes

  • Branch off the spinal nerve to communicate with the autonomic nervous system
  • Connect to sympathetic trunk ganglia
Path of a Spinal Nerve

From Cord to Periphery

Cord segment → Rootlets → Roots (dorsal sensory + ventral motor) → Spinal nerve (mixed) → Intervertebral foramen → Posterior & anterior rami → Plexus or direct branches → Target

Recall from the cord notes: because the cord ends at L1 to L2 but the column continues, lumbar, sacral, and coccygeal roots travel inferiorly inside the canal as the cauda equina before reaching their respective intervertebral foramina.

Anterior rami above and below the thoracic region do not run straight to a target. They form networks called plexuses, then send out named nerves. The cervical plexus drives the diaphragm; the brachial plexus drives the entire upper limb.

The Plexus Concept

What a Plexus Is

A plexus is a network of axons from the anterior rami of multiple spinal nerves. Axons from adjacent levels intermingle, then emerge as named nerves that supply specific regions.

Five Principal Plexuses

  1. Cervical plexus
  2. Brachial plexus
  3. Lumbar plexus
  4. Sacral plexus
  5. Coccygeal plexus

The named nerves emerging from a plexus are typically named for the region they serve.

Why bother with networks? Plexuses provide redundancy. If one spinal nerve root is damaged, the named peripheral nerves it contributes to often still function (with deficits) because they receive input from multiple roots. This is why complete sensory loss from a single root injury is rare.
T2 to T12 Are Different

Intercostal (Thoracic) Nerves

The anterior rami of T2 through T12 do not form plexuses. They run directly to their targets and are called intercostal nerves (or thoracic nerves).

Distribution

  • T2: innervates the second intercostal space muscles
  • T3 to T6: run along the costal grooves of the ribs; supply intercostal muscles and skin of the anterior chest wall
  • T7 to T12: supply intercostal muscles, abdominal muscles, and overlying skin

T1 contributes to the brachial plexus instead.

Cervical Plexus

Cervical Plexus (C1 to C5)

Formed by the anterior rami of C1 through C4, with some contributions from C5. One plexus on each side of the neck, alongside the first four cervical vertebrae.

Supplies

  • Skin and muscles of the head, neck, superior shoulders, and chest

Special Nerve: Phrenic

  • Origin: C3, C4, C5
  • Target: diaphragm
  • Significance: the only motor supply to the diaphragm; injury halts breathing

Clinical Significance

Complete cord transection above the origin of the phrenic nerves (above C3) causes respiratory arrest. The phrenic nerves can no longer send impulses to the diaphragm. Pressure from malignant tracheal or esophageal tumors in the mediastinum can also damage the phrenic nerves.

Mnemonic: "C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive."
Brachial Plexus

Brachial Plexus (C5 to T1)

Formed by the anterior rami of C5, C6, C7, C8, and T1. Passes above the first rib, posterior to the clavicle, and enters the axilla.

Five Levels of Organization

  1. Roots: the five anterior rami themselves
  2. Trunks: superior, middle, inferior
  3. Divisions: anterior and posterior
  4. Cords: lateral, medial, posterior
  5. Branches: the five terminal nerves
Mnemonic for the levels: Robert Taylor Drinks Cold Beer (Roots, Trunks, Divisions, Cords, Branches).

The branches of the brachial plexus provide almost the entire nerve supply of the shoulders and upper limbs.

Six classic brachial plexus injury patterns appear repeatedly on exams and in clinic. Each one has a signature posture, a signature mechanism, and a single nerve or root to blame. Learn the picture, learn the cause, learn the nerve.

Erb-Duchenne Palsy ("Waiter's Tip" Posture)

Injury location: superior roots of the brachial plexus (C5 to C6).

Mechanism: forceful pulling away of the head from the shoulder. Common scenarios include a heavy fall on the shoulder, an object falling onto the shoulder, or excessive stretching of an infant's neck during childbirth.

Presentation: the affected upper limb hangs in a characteristic posture. Shoulder is adducted, arm is medially rotated, elbow is extended, forearm is pronated, and wrist is flexed. The hand looks as if it is reaching back for a tip.

Sensory loss: along the lateral side of the arm.

Radial Nerve Injury

Radial Nerve Palsy ("Wrist Drop")

Mechanism

  • Improperly administered IM injection into the deltoid
  • Improperly applied or overly tight cast around the mid humerus
  • "Saturday night palsy" from prolonged compression of the arm

Presentation

  • Inability to extend the wrist and fingers
  • Wrist hangs in flexion (the "drop")
  • Sensory loss is minimal because adjacent nerves overlap in their cutaneous distribution
Median Nerve Injury

Median Nerve Palsy ("Ape Hand" / Hand of Benediction)

Mechanism

  • Injury to the median nerve, often at the wrist or in the forearm
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome is a chronic compressive form

Presentation

  • Numbness, tingling, and pain in the palm and fingers (lateral palm and digits 1 to 3)
  • Inability to pronate the forearm
  • Inability to flex the PIP joints of all digits and the DIP joints of the second and third digits
  • Weak wrist flexion and weak thumb movement
  • Thumb cannot oppose the other fingers ("ape hand")
Ulnar Nerve Injury

Ulnar Nerve Palsy ("Claw Hand")

Mechanism

  • Trauma at the medial epicondyle of the humerus (the "funny bone" area)
  • Compression at the elbow or wrist

Presentation

  • Inability to abduct or adduct the fingers
  • Atrophy of the interosseous muscles of the hand
  • Hyperextension of the metacarpophalangeal joints with flexion of the interphalangeal joints, especially in digits 4 and 5
  • Sensory loss in the medial palm and digits 4 and 5
Long Thoracic Nerve Injury

Winged Scapula

Mechanism

  • Injury to the long thoracic nerve
  • Causes paralysis of the serratus anterior muscle
  • Common with axillary surgery, chest tube placement, blunt trauma, or repetitive overhead activity

Presentation

  • The medial border of the scapula protrudes posteriorly, giving the appearance of a wing
  • Most obvious when the patient pushes against a wall with outstretched arms
Brachial Plexus Compression

Thoracic Outlet Syndrome

Mechanism

  • Compression of the brachial plexus and the subclavian artery or vein at the thoracic outlet
  • Sources include cervical rib, anterior scalene compression or spasm, clavicle fracture callus, or postural changes

Presentation

  • Pain, numbness, weakness, and tingling in the upper limb
  • Symptoms may radiate across the thorax and over the scapula on the affected side
  • May include vascular symptoms (color or temperature change in the limb)

The lower-body plexuses follow the same logic as the upper. Anterior rami merge, then send named nerves to specific regions. Two stand out: the femoral nerve (powerhouse of the anterior thigh) and the sciatic nerve (the largest nerve in the body).

Lumbar Plexus

Lumbar Plexus (L1 to L4)

Formed by the anterior rami of L1 through L4. Located alongside the four lumbar vertebrae, between the two heads of the psoas major muscle. Minimal intermingling of fibers compared with the brachial plexus.

Supplies

  • Anterolateral abdominal wall
  • External genitals
  • Part of the lower limbs

Major Nerves

  • Femoral nerve: anterior thigh muscles (quadriceps), skin of the anteromedial thigh and medial leg
  • Obturator nerve: medial thigh adductor muscles, skin of the medial thigh
  • Iliohypogastric, ilioinguinal, genitofemoral: abdominal wall and genital region
  • Lateral femoral cutaneous: skin of the lateral thigh
Sacral Plexus

Sacral Plexus (L4 to S4)

Formed by the anterior rami of L4 and L5 together with S1 through S4. Located just anterior to the sacrum.

Supplies

  • Buttocks
  • Perineum
  • Lower limbs

Special Structure: Sciatic Nerve

  • The largest nerve in the body
  • Actually two nerves bundled together by a common connective tissue sheath:
    • Tibial nerve
    • Common fibular (peroneal) nerve
  • The two components separate as the sciatic descends, typically near the popliteal fossa behind the knee
Coccygeal Plexus

Coccygeal Plexus (S4 to Co1)

The smallest plexus. Formed by the anterior rami of S4 and S5 and the coccygeal nerves.

Special Structure: Anococcygeal Nerves

  • Supply the skin over the coccyx

Clinical: Femoral Nerve Injury

Mechanism: most commonly stab or gunshot wounds. Pelvic fractures, hip surgery, and prolonged retraction during abdominal surgery are also causes.

Presentation:

  • Motor: inability to extend the leg at the knee (loss of quadriceps function)
  • Sensory: loss of sensation over the anteromedial aspect of the thigh

Clinical: Obturator Nerve Injury

Mechanism: often pressure on the nerve from the fetal head during pregnancy. Pelvic surgery and pelvic tumors can also injure the obturator.

Presentation:

  • Motor: paralysis of the adductor muscles of the thigh
  • Sensory: loss of sensation over the medial aspect of the thigh

Clinical: Sciatica

Definition: pain along the distribution of the sciatic nerve, typically extending from the buttock down the posterior and lateral aspect of the leg and foot.

Causes:

  1. Disc herniation (most common)
  2. Hip dislocation
  3. Osteoarthritis of the lumbosacral spine
  4. Pathological shortening of the lateral rotator muscles of the thigh, especially the piriformis (piriformis syndrome)
  5. Pressure from the uterus during pregnancy
  6. Local inflammation or irritation
  7. Improperly administered gluteal IM injection

Why gluteal IM injections must avoid the medial buttock: the sciatic nerve runs through the gluteal region, and a misplaced injection can damage it directly. Injections are placed in the upper outer quadrant of the buttock to stay clear of the nerve.

Each spinal nerve carries sensory information from a specific patch of skin. Map those patches across the body and you have a dermatome map. A few landmark dermatomes are worth knowing cold; they let you localize a cord or root injury at the bedside.

Definition

What a Dermatome Is

The skin over the entire body is supplied by somatic sensory neurons that carry impulses from the skin to the cord. Each spinal nerve contains sensory neurons that serve a specific region of skin. That region is called a dermatome.

Adjacent dermatomes overlap, which is why injury to a single spinal nerve rarely produces complete sensory loss in any one area. The exception is the trigeminal nerve territory of the face, which is served by cranial nerve V rather than spinal nerves.

Study Anchors

If You Remember Nothing Else

  • 31 pairs of spinal nerves, all mixed (sensory and motor).
  • Posterior root: sensory. Anterior root: motor. Cell bodies of sensory neurons live in the dorsal root ganglion.
  • Connective tissue wrapping, inside out: endoneurium → perineurium → epineurium.
  • Anterior rami of T2 to T12 become intercostal nerves; everywhere else they form plexuses.
  • Cervical plexus C1 to C5: phrenic nerve from C3, C4, C5.
  • Brachial plexus C5 to T1: 5 terminal branches (axillary, musculocutaneous, radial, median, ulnar).
  • Lumbar plexus L1 to L4: femoral and obturator nerves.
  • Sacral plexus L4 to S4: sciatic nerve = tibial + common fibular wrapped together.
  • Coccygeal plexus S4 to Co1: anococcygeal nerves.
  • Three trunk dermatome anchors: T4 nipple, T10 umbilicus, L1 inguinal.
