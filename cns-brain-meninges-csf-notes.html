Anatomy & Physiology Notes

CNS: Brain, Meninges & CSF

Comprehensive teaching notes on brain anatomy, protective coverings, ventricular system, and cerebrospinal fluid, organized for active studying.

Big Picture

The brain develops from a simple ectodermal neural tube into the most complex organ in the body. Knowing the embryologic origin of each region helps you remember what's connected to what in the adult brain.

Embryology

Brain Development: From Neural Tube to Adult Structures

The brain and spinal cord both develop from the ectodermal neural tube. The anterior end of the tube expands and differentiates first into 3 primary brain vesicles, then into 5 secondary vesicles, then into the adult brain regions.

Primary Brain Vesicles (3)

  1. Prosencephalon (forebrain)
  2. Mesencephalon (midbrain)
  3. Rhombencephalon (hindbrain)

Secondary Brain Vesicles (5)

Prosencephalon splits into Telencephalon + Diencephalon. Rhombencephalon splits into Metencephalon + Myelencephalon. Mesencephalon stays as itself.

Adult Structures from Each Secondary Vesicle

Secondary VesicleAdult Structure(s)
TelencephalonCerebrum, lateral ventricles
DiencephalonThalamus, hypothalamus, epithalamus, third ventricle
MesencephalonMidbrain, cerebral aqueduct
MetencephalonPons, cerebellum, upper part of fourth ventricle
MyelencephalonMedulla oblongata, lower part of fourth ventricle

Foundation

The Four Major Parts of the Brain

  1. Brainstem. Connects the rest of the brain to the spinal cord. Made of midbrain, pons, and medulla oblongata.
  2. Cerebellum. Coordinates skilled movements, regulates posture and balance.
  3. Diencephalon. Links the cerebral hemispheres to the brainstem. Contains thalamus, hypothalamus, and epithalamus.
  4. Cerebrum. The largest part. Where you see, hear, smell, taste, feel, think, and decide.
As you learn each structure, ask which of these four major parts it lives in. The geography keeps the details organized.

Speed vs. Adaptability

Spinal Cord vs. Brain

  • Spinal cord: reflex. Immediate response.
  • Brain: thinking. Adaptable response, slower.

The spinal cord handles fast, automatic responses (reflexes). The brain takes longer because it's evaluating, weighing, and choosing among options.

Meninges & Blood-Brain Barrier

The brain is too important to leave unprotected. Three layers of meninges, dural folds that compartmentalize the brain regions, and a tightly controlled blood-brain barrier all work together to keep the CNS safe.

Three Protective Layers

Cranial Meninges

From outermost to innermost:

1. Dura Mater (Outermost)

  • Two layers: the periosteal layer (attached to inner skull) and the meningeal layer (internal). The two layers are fused together except at dural venous sinuses, which drain venous blood into the internal jugular veins.
  • No epidural space in the cranial cavity. The dura is fused to the periosteum.

2. Arachnoid Mater (Middle)

  • Lace-like membrane.
  • Arachnoid trabeculae span the subarachnoid space, which contains CSF.

3. Pia Mater (Innermost)

  • Adheres directly to brain surface (held by astrocytes).
  • Follows the topology of gyri and sulci into every fold.
  • Contains blood vessels.

Dural Compartmentalization

Dural Reflections (Folds)

Three extensions of dura mater divide the cranial cavity and stabilize the brain regions:

  1. Falx Cerebri. Separates the left and right cerebral hemispheres. Sits in the longitudinal fissure.
  2. Falx Cerebelli. Separates the left and right cerebellar hemispheres.
  3. Tentorium Cerebelli. Separates the cerebrum (above) from the cerebellum (below).
Memory cue: falx means sickle (the shape). Tentorium means tent (it tents over the cerebellum).

Selective Permeability Barrier

Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB)

The BBB is a selective permeability barrier that protects the brain's extracellular environment from fluctuations in plasma composition, toxins, and pathogens, while still allowing essential substances to pass.

Structural Components

  1. Capillary endothelial cells joined by tight junctions. No fenestrations (unlike typical capillaries).
  2. Basement membrane surrounding the endothelium. Adds filtration and structural support.
  3. Astrocyte end-feet envelop the capillaries. Regulate and support endothelial function.

How Things Cross the BBB

  • Passive diffusion: gases (O₂, CO₂), lipid-soluble substances (alcohol, nicotine, some anesthetics).
  • Facilitated diffusion: glucose (via GLUT1), amino acids.
  • Active transport: ions, vitamins, some drugs.
  • Receptor-mediated transcytosis: insulin, transferrin.

Functions

  • Maintains CNS homeostasis (ion composition, pH).
  • Protects neural tissue from pathogens and toxins.
  • Prevents neurotransmitter leakage into systemic circulation.
  • Regulates nutrient and waste exchange.
When the BBB breaks down (infection, cancer, immune disorders), the brain becomes vulnerable. Many drugs that should reach the brain can't cross the BBB, which is part of why some neurologic diseases are so hard to treat.

Special Exceptions

Areas Without a Typical BBB

Some regions need to either sense plasma composition or release hormones into the blood, so they have a leaky barrier. These are the circumventricular organs:

  • Median eminence (hypothalamic hormone release)
  • Posterior pituitary
  • Pineal gland
  • Area postrema (the vomiting center, detects toxins in the blood)

The area postrema is why ingesting a toxin can trigger vomiting. The brain literally tastes the blood there.

Ventricles & Cerebrospinal Fluid

CSF is the brain's bath. It cushions, transports, and removes waste. The ventricular system makes it, circulates it, and returns it to venous blood. Knowing the flow path is essential for understanding hydrocephalus and lumbar puncture.

CSF-Filled Cavities

The Four Ventricles

Interconnected CSF-filled chambers lined by ependymal cells.

  1. Two Lateral Ventricles. One in each cerebral hemisphere. Separated by the septum pellucidum.
  2. Third Ventricle. Midline, in the diencephalon. Just above the hypothalamus, between the right and left halves of the thalamus.
  3. Fourth Ventricle. Between the brainstem (pons) and the cerebellum. Continuous with the central canal of the spinal cord below.

CSF Production

Choroid Plexus & CSF Formation

The choroid plexus makes the majority of CSF (about 500 mL per day).

Structure

Networks of blood capillaries covered by ependymal cells with tight junctions. The ependymal cells secrete the filtered CSF.

Composition

Clear, colorless fluid composed mostly of water. Carries glucose, oxygen, electrolytes (cations and anions), proteins, and urea.

Turnover

The total volume of CSF in brain and cord is replaced every 8 hours.

CSF Pathway

Flow of CSF Through the CNS

What CSF Does

Functions of CSF

  1. Mechanical protection. Shock-absorbing medium. Reduces effective brain weight by buoyancy.
  2. Homeostasis. pH affects pulmonary ventilation and blood flow. Maintains ionic balance.
  3. Circulation. Medium for exchange of nutrients and waste products.
Buoyancy is real. The brain weighs about 1400 g in air but only about 50 g floating in CSF. Without CSF, the brain would crush itself under its own weight.

Clinical Correlation: Hydrocephalus

Definition: Abnormal accumulation of CSF in the ventricles, leading to ventricular enlargement and increased intracranial pressure.

Cause: Failure to reabsorb CSF, or obstruction of flow.

In infants: The cranium expands because the sutures haven't fused. The fluid expands the cranium and crushes the brain.

In adults: Brain compression. Possible herniation if untreated.

Cerebrum

The cerebrum is the largest and most complex part of the brain, the seat of intelligence, perception, voluntary movement, memory, and emotion. Its surface folds (gyri and sulci) increase the surface area available for the 6 billion neurons of the cortex.

Foundation

Cerebrum Overview

General Features

  • Largest region of the brain.
  • Two cerebral hemispheres connected by the corpus callosum.
  • Each hemisphere has 5 lobes: frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital, and insula.
  • Sits on the diencephalon and brainstem, filling most of the cranial vault.

Cerebral Cortex (Outer Layer)

  • 2 to 4 mm thick layer of gray matter.
  • Contains about 6 billion neuronal cell bodies and dendrites.
  • Where intelligence, perception, voluntary movement, and sensation happen.

Cerebral White Matter (Beneath Cortex)

  • Made up of myelinated axons traveling to and from the cortex.
  • Sometimes called the internal capsule.

Surface Anatomy

Gyri, Sulci & Fissures

The cortex is highly folded to maximize surface area for neurons.

  • Gyri: elevated ridges of cortex.
  • Sulci: shallow grooves between gyri.
  • Fissures: deeper grooves dividing major regions.

Key Sulci & Fissures

  • Central sulcus: separates frontal from parietal lobes. Divides the primary motor cortex (anterior) from the primary somatosensory cortex (posterior).
  • Lateral sulcus: separates frontal and temporal lobes.
  • Parieto-occipital sulcus: separates parietal and occipital lobes.
  • Longitudinal fissure: divides left and right cerebral hemispheres.
  • Transverse fissure: separates cerebrum from cerebellum.

Five Lobes

Cerebral Lobes

Each lobe is named for the cranial bone covering it (except the insula, which is hidden).

LobePrimary Functions
FrontalMotor planning, voluntary movement (precentral gyrus), speech production (Broca's), personality, judgment
ParietalSomatic sensation (postcentral gyrus), spatial orientation
TemporalHearing, language comprehension (Wernicke's), memory
OccipitalVisual processing
InsulaTaste, visceral sensation, emotion, autonomic integration. Hidden deep within the lateral fissure, beneath frontal, parietal, and temporal lobes.

White Matter Tracts

Three Types of Cerebral White Matter Fibers

  1. Association fibers. Connect gyri within the same hemisphere. Allow intra-hemispheric communication. Example: arcuate fasciculus connects Broca's and Wernicke's areas.
  2. Commissural fibers. Connect matching gyri between hemispheres. The corpus callosum is the largest. Others: anterior commissure, posterior commissure.
  3. Projection fibers. Connect cortex to lower brain regions or spinal cord. Ascending (sensory) or descending (motor) tracts. The internal capsule is the major projection pathway near the basal ganglia.

Gray Matter Nuclei

Basal Ganglia (Cerebral Nuclei)

Several groups of paired nuclei found within the white matter of the cerebrum. Interconnected with each other and receiving inputs from the cerebral cortex, thalamus, and hypothalamus.

Components

  • Caudate nucleus
  • Putamen
  • Globus pallidus
  • Functionally also includes substantia nigra and subthalamic nucleus

Functions

  • Control of large, learned, semi-voluntary skeletal muscle movements (arm swinging while walking, laughing).
  • Initiation and regulation of motor activity.
  • Suppression of unwanted movement.
  • Modulation of muscle tone.

Three Famous Areas

Integrative Centers

Wernicke's Area

Location: Left hemisphere, near auditory and visual cortices.

Function: Language comprehension. Gives meaning to auditory and visual cues.

Damage: Receptive aphasia (can speak fluently but with nonsensical meaning).

Broca's Area

Location: Left frontal lobe, superior to lateral sulcus.

Function: Coordinates motor control of speech.

Damage: Expressive aphasia (difficulty forming speech, comprehension intact).

Prefrontal Cortex

Location: Anterior frontal lobe.

Function: Judgment, prediction, planning, decision-making, emotional regulation, personality, social behavior.

Right Brain, Left Brain

Hemispheric Specialization

Left Hemisphere

Language, logic, math, reasoning.

Right Hemisphere

Emotion, visual-spatial skills, music, face recognition.

Contralateral Control

Each hemisphere processes and controls the opposite side of the body.

Corpus Callosum

The major commissural fiber that connects the two hemispheres. If severed (sometimes done as an epilepsy treatment), each hemisphere functions independently.

Functional System

Limbic System

Definition: A functional region (not anatomical) spanning parts of the cerebrum and diencephalon.

Main Functions

  1. Establishes emotional states and motivational drives.
  2. Links conscious cerebral functions to unconscious brainstem responses.
  3. Facilitates long-term memory storage and retrieval.

Key Structures

  • Amygdala: fear and aggression. Initiates "fight or flight." Links emotion to memory.
  • Hippocampus: memory and learning.
  • Cingulate gyrus: expresses emotion through gestures, conflict resolution.
"Limbic" comes from limbus, meaning border or edge. The system forms a border around the brainstem, linking thinking (cortex) and feeling (deeper structures).

Clinical Correlation: Stroke (CVA)

Definition: Sudden loss of blood flow to a portion of the brain.

Cause: Thrombus, embolus, or hemorrhage.

Effect: Neuronal death within minutes due to oxygen deprivation. Consequences depend on the affected area: paralysis, aphasia, vision loss, etc.

Clinical Correlation: Epileptic Seizures

Definition: Abnormal, excessive electrical activity in the cortex causing loss of normal function.

Symptoms: Vary widely. Motor convulsions, sensory disturbances, loss of consciousness.

Often associated with disruptions in functional area regulation.

Clinical Correlation: Anencephaly

Definition: Neural tube defect in which the brain fails to develop above the midbrain. Major portions of the cerebrum and skull are absent.

Outcome: Always fatal. Often diagnosed prenatally.

Cerebellum & Diencephalon

The cerebellum is the brain's coordinator, comparing what you intended to do with what your body actually did. The diencephalon is the relay and homeostatic command center, sitting between the cerebrum and the brainstem.

Movement Coordinator

Cerebellum: Structure

Second largest brain region. Located in the posterior cranial fossa, inferior to the occipital lobes and posterior to the brainstem. Separated from the cerebrum by the tentorium cerebelli.

Structural Features

  • Two cerebellar hemispheres connected by the vermis.
  • Three lobes per hemisphere: anterior, posterior, and flocculonodular.
  • Surface features: folia (fine, leaf-like folds), cerebellar cortex (gray matter), arbor vitae (white matter tracts in a tree-like pattern), and deep cerebellar nuclei (such as the dentate).

Cerebellar Peduncles (Connect to Brainstem)

  • Superior cerebellar peduncle: connects to midbrain. Mostly efferent.
  • Middle cerebellar peduncle: connects to pons. Afferent from pontine nuclei.
  • Inferior cerebellar peduncle: connects to medulla. Afferent from spinal cord and vestibular system.

Cerebellar Functions

What the Cerebellum Does

  • Coordination of voluntary movements. Compares intended motor output (from motor cortex) with actual movement (from proprioceptive input).
  • Balance and posture. Integrates vestibular, visual, and proprioceptive input.
  • Motor learning. Learning sequences of skilled movements (playing piano, riding a bike).
  • Regulation of muscle tone.
The cerebellum doesn't generate movement, it refines it. The motor cortex says "move arm." The cerebellum says "smoothly, with the right speed and force, while keeping balance."

Clinical Correlations: Cerebellar Dysfunction

Ataxia: uncoordinated movements due to cerebellar dysfunction.

Intention tremor: oscillations during voluntary movement (worse as the hand approaches a target).

Nystagmus: abnormal eye movements from flocculonodular lobe lesions.

Hypotonia: decreased muscle tone from deep nuclei damage.

Three-Part Region

Diencephalon Overview

Links the cerebral hemispheres to the brainstem. Surrounds the third ventricle. Three components:

  1. Thalamus (about 80% of the diencephalon)
  2. Hypothalamus (about 20%)
  3. Epithalamus (small, posterior)

Sensory Relay

Thalamus

"Inner chamber." Oval structure above the midbrain. Forms about 4/5 of the diencephalon.

Structure

Paired masses of gray matter organized into nuclei, forming the lateral walls of the third ventricle. The two halves are joined by the massa intermedia (interthalamic adhesion).

Function

The principal relay station for sensory impulses (except smell) reaching the cerebral cortex from the spinal cord, brainstem, and other parts of the cerebrum.

Other Roles

  • Crude appreciation of some sensations (pain, temperature, pressure).
  • Some role in emotions and memory.
  • Each thalamic nucleus is specific for a particular modality.

Master Homeostatic Regulator

Hypothalamus

Lies inferior to the thalamus. About 1/5 of the diencephalon. Composed of paired nuclei (mostly gray matter).

Why It Matters

Each nucleus pair controls a specific homeostatic function. The hypothalamus is the major controller of homeostasis and the primary integration point between the nervous and endocrine systems.

Eight Major Functions

  1. Integration between nervous and endocrine systems (via pituitary).
  2. Control of the autonomic nervous system.
  3. Regulation of rage and aggression.
  4. Regulation of body temperature.
  5. Regulation of food intake.
  6. Regulation of thirst.
  7. Regulation of sleep-wake patterns (circadian rhythms).
  8. Regulation of sex drive.

Smallest Component

Epithalamus

  • Contains the pineal gland, which secretes melatonin for circadian rhythm regulation.
  • Includes the habenular nuclei, involved in emotional responses to odors.

Brainstem

The brainstem is small but does enormous work. It connects the cerebrum, diencephalon, and cerebellum to the spinal cord. It controls the reflexes that keep you alive (breathing, heart rate, swallowing). It houses 10 of the 12 cranial nerve nuclei. And it's the throughway for everything ascending and descending between brain and body.

Three-Part Connector

Brainstem Overview

Location: Between the brain and the spinal cord. Continuous with the inferior spinal cord and the superior pons. Lies just inside the cranial vault, superior to the foramen magnum.

Three Parts (top to bottom)

  1. Midbrain (mesencephalon)
  2. Pons
  3. Medulla oblongata

Top of Brainstem

Midbrain (Mesencephalon)

Extends from the superior portion of the pons to the inferior border of the diencephalon.

Cerebral Aqueduct

Passes through the midbrain. Connects the third and fourth ventricles.

Dorsal Portion: Tectum (Roof)

Divided into four rounded eminences of gray matter called the corpus quadrigemina:

  • Superior colliculi (2): reflex movements of eyes, head, and neck in response to visual and other stimuli (startle reflex).
  • Inferior colliculi (2): reflex movements of head and trunk in response to auditory stimuli.

Ventral Portion

All white matter. Two groups of fiber tracts:

  • Cerebral peduncles: pass motor fibers from cerebrum through the brainstem. Also carry some sensory information.
  • Medial lemniscus: band of sensory tracts passing from lower CNS to thalamus. Carries discriminative touch, proprioception, pressure, and vibration.

Substantia Nigra

Dopamine-releasing neurons that control muscle activities. (Degeneration here causes Parkinson's disease.)

Cranial Nerve Nuclei

CN III (Oculomotor), CN IV (Trochlear).

Middle Brainstem

Pons

Bridge between medulla, midbrain, and cerebellum. Lies directly superior to the medulla, anterior to the cerebellum, inferior to the midbrain.

Two Functional Components

  • Dorsal: ascending and descending tracts (similar to the rest of the brainstem).
  • Ventral: relay station with pontine nuclei. Contains the pontine respiratory group that helps control breathing.

Histology

  • White matter (dorsal): tracts passing between lower and higher CNS structures. Communicates cerebellum with the rest of the CNS.
  • Gray matter (ventral): two nuclei involved in altering the basic respiratory pattern.

Cranial Nerve Nuclei

CN V (Trigeminal), CN VI (Abducens), CN VII (Facial), and part of CN VIII (Vestibulocochlear).

Bottom of Brainstem

Medulla Oblongata

The most inferior part of the brainstem. Continuous with the spinal cord at the foramen magnum.

Tracts & Decussation

  • Carries ascending (sensory) and descending (motor) tracts.
  • Tracts cross at the decussation of pyramids. This is why each side of the brain controls voluntary movements on the opposite side of the body.

Vital Reflex Centers (in Gray Matter)

Nuclei that control processes necessary for immediate survival:

  • Cardiovascular center (blood pressure, heart rate)
  • Respiratory center
  • Vomiting center
  • Swallowing, coughing

White Matter

All ascending and descending tracts that communicate between brain and spinal cord pass through the medulla.

Cranial Nerve Nuclei

CN VIII (Vestibulocochlear, part), CN IX (Glossopharyngeal), CN X (Vagus), CN XI (Accessory, cranial portion), CN XII (Hypoglossal).

Sensory neuron cell bodies for these cranial nerves are located outside the medulla in various ganglia.

Throughout the Brainstem

Reticular Formation & the RAS

Definition: A network of gray matter cell bodies interspersed within white matter tracts, extending throughout the brainstem.

Ascending Reticular Activating System (ARAS or RAS)

  • Sensory axons that ascend through the thalamus to the cerebral cortex.
  • Stimuli integrated: visual, auditory, touch, pain, pressure.
  • Most important function: maintains consciousness and alertness.
  • Active during arousal and wakefulness.
  • Maintains attention.
  • Filters incoming stimuli to prevent sensory overload.
  • Inactivation leads to sleep or coma.

Descending Component

  • Modulates muscle tone of resting skeletal muscles.
  • Coordinates somatic motor functions with autonomic reflexes.
When you're trying to fall asleep with the TV on, your RAS is what's keeping you awake. When the RAS quiets down, you sleep. When it shuts off entirely, you're in a coma.
