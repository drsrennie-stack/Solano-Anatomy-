Female Reproductive System

Comprehensive teaching notes covering structure, function, and clinical relevance, organized for active studying.

Big Picture

The female reproductive system has six major organs working together to produce gametes, host fertilization and pregnancy, and nourish a newborn. Each structure has a defined job. Learn the role first, then the anatomy makes sense.

Foundation

The Six Organs of the Female Reproductive System

  1. Ovaries (gonads). Produce gametes (secondary oocytes) and hormones (estrogens, progesterone, inhibin, relaxin).
  2. Uterine (fallopian) tubes. Transport secondary oocytes from ovaries to uterus. Site of fertilization.
  3. Uterus. Site of implantation, fetal development, and labor.
  4. Vagina. Receptacle for the penis during intercourse, outlet for menstrual flow, and passageway for childbirth.
  5. External organs (vulva or pudendum). Protective structures and sensory organs for sexual response.
  6. Mammary glands. Synthesize, secrete, and eject milk for nourishment of the newborn.
Note: mammary glands belong to both the reproductive and integumentary systems. Keep that in mind when studying skin too.

Ovaries & Oogenesis

Unlike sperm production which begins at puberty, oogenesis begins before birth. A baby girl is born with all the primary oocytes she will ever have. Only about 400 of those 1 to 2 million primary oocytes will mature and ovulate during her reproductive lifetime.

Female Gonad

Ovaries

Definition: Female gonads (homologous to testes). Almond sized and shaped. Paired.

Location

One on each side of the uterus. Descend into the brim of the superior pelvic cavity during the third month of development.

Functions

  1. Production of gametes (secondary oocytes that develop into mature ova after fertilization).
  2. Production of hormones: progesterone, estrogens, inhibin, and relaxin.

Hilum: each ovary has a hilum (point of entry/exit for blood vessels and nerves), where the mesovarium attaches.

Anchor & Suspension

Supporting Ligaments of the Ovaries

  1. Broad ligament of the uterus. Fold of parietal peritoneum. Attaches to the ovaries by a double-layered fold of peritoneum called the mesovarium.
  2. Ovarian ligament. Anchors the ovaries to the uterus.
  3. Suspensory ligament. Attaches ovaries to the pelvic wall. Contains ovarian blood vessels and nerves.

Histology

Histology of the Ovary

From superficial to deep:

  1. Germinal epithelium. Single layer of simple cuboidal or squamous epithelium. Important: despite the name, this layer does NOT give rise to ova. The cells that produce ova arise from the yolk sac and migrate to the ovaries during embryonic development.
  2. Tunica albuginea. Whitish capsule of dense irregular CT immediately deep to the germinal epithelium.
  3. Ovarian cortex. Just deep to the tunica albuginea. Contains ovarian follicles surrounded by dense irregular CT with collagen fibers and fibroblast-like stromal cells.
  4. Ovarian medulla. Deep to the cortex (indistinct border between them). More loose CT. Contains blood vessels, lymphatic vessels, and nerves.
  5. Ovarian follicles. Found in the cortex. Contain oocytes in various stages of development. Surrounding cells start as a single layer of follicular cells; later development produces several layers of granulosa cells. These cells nourish the developing oocyte and begin to secrete estrogens as the follicle grows.
  6. Mature follicle. Large fluid-filled follicle ready to rupture and expel its secondary oocyte (ovulation).
  7. Corpus luteum (yellow body). Remnants of a mature follicle after ovulation. Produces progesterone, estrogens, relaxin, and inhibin until it degenerates into the corpus albicans (white body), a fibrous scar.

Key Comparison

Oogenesis vs. Spermatogenesis

Timing

  • Males: begin at puberty.
  • Females: begin before birth, during embryonic development.

Final Output Per Primary Cell

  • Spermatogenesis: 1 primary spermatocyte → 4 sperm.
  • Oogenesis: 1 primary oocyte → 1 ovum + 3 polar bodies (which all degenerate).

Cell Type

Both involve meiosis with subsequent maturation. The female version distributes most of the cytoplasm into one cell (the ovum) so that cell carries enough resources to support early embryonic development.

Before Birth

Oogenesis: Early Fetal Development

  1. Primordial germ cells migrate from the yolk sac to the ovaries.
  2. Within the ovaries, they differentiate into oogonia: diploid (2n) stem cells. These divide via mitosis to produce millions of germ cells.
  3. Before birth, most degenerate (atresia).
  4. A few develop into larger cells called primary oocytes.
  5. Primary oocytes enter Meiosis I during fetal development but do not complete it until after puberty (arrested stage).
  6. Each primary oocyte is surrounded by a flat layer of follicular cells. The whole structure is a primordial follicle.

By the Numbers

  • At birth: 200,000 to 2,000,000 primary oocytes per ovary
  • At puberty: about 40,000 still present
  • Across the lifetime: only about 400 will mature and ovulate. The rest undergo atresia.

After Puberty

Oogenesis: Follicular Development

Each month, FSH and LH from the anterior pituitary stimulate development of several primordial follicles. Typically only one reaches maturity needed for ovulation.

Stage 1: Primordial Follicle

Primary oocyte arrested in prophase I, surrounded by a single layer of flattened follicular cells.

Stage 2: Primary Follicle

  • Primary oocyte at a later stage of development.
  • Surrounded by several layers of cuboidal and low-columnar cells called granulosa cells.
  • Outermost granulosa cells rest on a basement membrane.
  • Forms a clear glycoprotein layer called the zona pellucida between the primary oocyte and granulosa cells.
  • Stromal cells around the basement membrane organize into the theca folliculi.

Stage 3: Secondary Follicle

  • Theca differentiates into two layers:
    • Theca interna: highly vascular, cuboidal secretory cells. Secrete estrogens.
    • Theca externa: outer layer of stromal cells and collagen fibers.
  • Granulosa cells secrete follicular fluid that builds up in a cavity called the antrum.
  • The innermost layer of granulosa cells firmly attaches to the zona pellucida and is now called the corona radiata.

Stage 4: Mature (Graafian) Follicle

  • Large fluid-filled follicle just prior to ovulation.
  • The primary oocyte completes Meiosis I, producing two haploid (n) cells of unequal size:
    • First polar body: the smaller cell, a packet of disorganized material.
    • Secondary oocyte: larger cell, receives most of the cytoplasm. Begins Meiosis II and stops in metaphase.
  • The mature follicle ruptures and releases the secondary oocyte. This is ovulation.
Important: Meiosis II is only completed if fertilization occurs. The secondary oocyte sits paused in metaphase II until a sperm arrives.

After Ovulation

Corpus Luteum & Corpus Albicans

Corpus Luteum (Yellow Body)

What's left of the mature follicle after ovulation. It is a hormone factory.

Hormones produced: progesterone, estrogens, relaxin, inhibin.

Corpus Albicans (White Body)

If fertilization does not occur, the corpus luteum degenerates into a fibrous scar called the corpus albicans. This is the end of the road for that follicle.

Ovulation, Fertilization & Uterine Tubes

Fertilization typically happens in the ampulla of the uterine tube, not in the ovary or uterus. The uterine tubes are designed to capture an ovulated oocyte and shuttle it (and any zygote) toward the uterus.

Two Outcomes

Ovulation & Fertilization: Step by Step

What Comes Out of the Mature Follicle

The secondary oocyte is expelled into the pelvic cavity along with the first polar body and the corona radiata. Fimbriae sweep it into the uterine tube.

If Fertilization Does NOT Occur

The cells degenerate. The cycle ends. Menstruation will follow.

If Sperm Are Present

  1. One sperm penetrates the secondary oocyte.
  2. This resumes meiosis II in the secondary oocyte.
  3. The secondary oocyte splits into two haploid cells of unequal size:
    • Larger cell = ovum (mature egg).
    • Smaller cell = second polar body.
  4. The nuclei of sperm and ovum unite to form a diploid zygote.
  5. The first polar body undergoes another division into two polar bodies.

Final Tally

One primary oocyte yields 1 ovum + 3 polar bodies (all of which degenerate). Compare to males: 1 primary spermatocyte yields 4 functional sperm.

The asymmetric division is intentional. The ovum needs all the cytoplasm to support a developing embryo. The polar bodies just take the extra chromosomes.

Fertilization Timeline

From Ovulation to Implantation

  1. After ovulation, currents from the fimbriae surround the mature follicle.
  2. Currents sweep the ovulated secondary oocyte from the peritoneal cavity into the uterine tube.
  3. A sperm cell typically encounters and fertilizes the secondary oocyte in the ampulla of the uterine tube. (Fertilization in the peritoneal cavity is not uncommon.)
  4. Fertilization can occur up to 24 hours after ovulation.
  5. Hours after fertilization, the haploid nuclei unite.
  6. The diploid fertilized ovum is now a zygote. It begins cell divisions while moving toward the uterus.
  7. Arrives in the uterus 6 to 7 days after ovulation.
  8. Unfertilized secondary oocytes disintegrate.

Egg Highway

Uterine Tubes (Fallopian Tubes)

Funnel-shaped, long tubes that extend laterally from the uterus.

Location

Lie within the folds of the broad ligament of the uterus. From the infundibulum, extend medially and eventually inferiorly. Attach to the superior lateral angle of the uterus.

Functions

  1. Provide a route for sperm to reach an ovum.
  2. Transport secondary oocytes and fertilized ova from ovaries to uterus.

Anatomy

Parts of the Uterine Tube

  1. Infundibulum. Funnel-shaped portion close to the ovary, open to the pelvic cavity.
  2. Fimbriae. Fringe of finger-like projections at the end of the infundibulum. Attached to the lateral end of the ovary.
  3. Ampulla. Widest and longest part of the uterine tube. Makes up the lateral 2/3 of its length. Site of fertilization.
  4. Isthmus. The medial, short, narrow, thick-walled portion that joins the uterus.

Mesosalpinx: a part of the broad ligament that supports the uterine tubes.

Histology

Three Layers of the Uterine Tubes

1. Mucosa

  • Ciliated simple columnar cells. Form a "ciliary conveyor belt" that helps move the fertilized ovum or secondary oocyte toward the uterus.
  • Non-ciliated cells (peg cells). Have microvilli. Secrete fluid that nourishes the ovum and supports sperm.
  • Lamina propria. Areolar CT.

2. Muscularis

Function: peristalsis (wave-like contractions) that move the ovum or secondary oocyte toward the uterus.

  • Inner layer: thick circular ring of smooth muscle.
  • Outer layer: thin region of longitudinal smooth muscle.

3. Serosa

Outer layer of serous membrane.

Uterus

The uterus is an inverted pear-shaped organ that does several jobs: it's part of the sperm pathway, it's the implantation site, it supports fetal development, it assists in labor, and it's the source of menstrual flow when implantation does not occur.

Five Jobs

Uterus Overview

Functions

  1. Part of the pathway for sperm deposited in the vagina (to reach the uterine tubes).
  2. Site of implantation of a fertilized ovum.
  3. Development of the fetus during pregnancy.
  4. Assists in labor.
  5. Source of menstrual flow during cycles when implantation has not occurred.

Characteristics

Inverted pear shape. Variable size depending on pregnancy (much larger). Smaller (atrophied) without hormone influence (menopause).

Location

Between the urinary bladder (anterior) and the rectum (posterior).

Position & Divisions

Position & Anatomical Divisions

Normal Position

  • Uterus: anteflexion. Projects anteriorly and superiorly.
  • Cervix: projects inferiorly and posteriorly. Enters the anterior wall of the vagina at a right angle.

Anatomical Divisions

  1. Fundus. Dome-shaped superior portion (above the openings of the uterine tubes).
  2. Body. Tapered central portion.
  3. Cervix. Inferior narrow portion that opens into the vagina.

Isthmus: constricted region between the body and the cervix.

Uterine cavity: interior of the body of the uterus.

Cervical canal: interior of the cervix. Opens into the uterine cavity at the internal os and into the vagina at the external os.

Retroflexion

Posterior tilting of the uterus. Harmless, no identifiable cause, may occur after childbirth.

Anchor System

Supporting Ligaments of the Uterus

These keep the uterus in its anteflexed position while still allowing some movement.

  1. Broad ligaments (paired). Double folds of peritoneum attaching to the uterus on either side of the pelvic cavity.
  2. Uterosacral ligaments (paired). Peritoneal extensions that lie on either side of the rectum and connect the uterus to the sacrum.
  3. Cardinal (lateral cervical) ligaments. Inferior to the bases of the broad ligaments. Extend from the pelvic wall to the cervix and vagina.
  4. Round ligaments. Bands of fibrous CT between the layers of the broad ligament. Extend from a point on the uterus, through the inguinal canal, to the labia majora of the external genitalia.

Histology

The Three Layers of the Uterus

1. Perimetrium (Outer Layer / Serosa)

Part of the visceral peritoneum. Made of simple squamous epithelium and areolar CT.

  • Laterally: becomes the broad ligament.
  • Anteriorly: covers the urinary bladder, forms a shallow pouch.
  • Posteriorly: covers the rectum and forms a deep pouch (the rectouterine pouch or pouch of Douglas). This is the most inferior point in the pelvic cavity.

2. Myometrium (Middle Layer)

Three layers of smooth muscle. Thickest in the fundus, thinnest in the cervix.

  • Inner and outer layers: longitudinal.
  • Middle layer: oblique.

Function: coordinated contractions during childbirth. Responds to oxytocin from the posterior pituitary to expel the fetus from the uterus.

3. Endometrium (Inner Layer)

Highly vascular. Three components:

  1. Innermost layer: simple columnar epithelium with cilia and secretory cells.
  2. Underlying stroma: very thick region of lamina propria (areolar CT).
  3. Uterine (endometrial) glands: invaginations of the luminal epithelium that extend almost into the myometrium.

Two Sub-Layers

Endometrium: Functionalis vs. Basalis

Stratum Functionalis

The functional layer that lines the uterine cavity. Sloughs off during menstruation. Thickens in response to hormones each cycle.

Stratum Basalis

The basal (permanent) layer. Gives rise to a new stratum functionalis after each menstruation. The regenerative engine of the endometrium.

Memory cue: functional = function for that cycle, then leaves. Basal = base, stays put.

Vascular Anatomy

Blood Supply to the Uterus

Internal iliac artery → Uterine arteries → Arcuate arteries (in myometrium) → Radial arteries (deeper in myometrium) → Two arterioles before the endometrium

Two Arterioles into the Endometrium

  • Straight arterioles. Supply the stratum basalis. They survive each menstrual cycle and enable regeneration of the functionalis.
  • Spiral arterioles. Supply the stratum functionalis. They change during the menstrual cycle and constrict to drive sloughing.

Drainage

Uterine veins drain into the internal iliac veins.

Why Such an Extensive Blood Supply

  1. Support the growth of a new stratum functionalis after menstruation.
  2. Implantation of a fertilized ovum.
  3. Development of the placenta.

Cervix & Vagina

Two structures, two distinct environments. The cervix produces a hormone-responsive mucus that changes the rules of sperm transit. The vagina is acidic by design, hostile to microbes but also to sperm.

Gateway

Cervix

The narrow, lower portion of the uterus that connects to the vagina.

Cervical Canal

The passage through the cervix that connects the uterus to the vagina. Opens into the uterine cavity at the internal os and into the vagina at the external os.

Mucosa

Cervical lining composed of simple columnar epithelium and mucus-secreting glands.

Hormone-Responsive Secretion

Cervical Mucus

Produced by: secretory cells of the mucosa of the cervix.

Composition: water, glycoproteins, lipids, enzymes, inorganic salts.

Volume: 20 to 60 mL/day during reproductive age.

Consistency Changes Across the Cycle

  • Near ovulation: less viscous, more alkaline, hospitable to sperm.
  • Other times: can impede sperm penetration.

Functions

  1. Supplements sperm with energy.
  2. Protects sperm from phagocytes and the hostile environment of the vagina/uterus.
  3. Supports capacitation: a series of functional changes sperm undergo in the female reproductive tract prior to fertilization.
    • Sperm tail beats more vigorously.
    • Plasma membrane is prepared to fuse with the oocyte.
Capacitation cannot occur in the male body. Sperm can only become fully fertilization-ready inside the female reproductive tract.

Three Functions

Vagina

Definition: Tubular, long fibromuscular canal lined with mucous membrane.

Functions

  • Receptacle for the penis during intercourse.
  • Outlet for menstrual flow.
  • Passageway for childbirth.

Location

Between the urinary bladder and the rectum. Sits superiorly and posteriorly. Attaches to the uterus.

Fornix

The arch or vault that surrounds the vaginal attachment to the cervix. (This is where a contraceptive diaphragm sits.) It covers the cervix.

Histology

Layers of the Vagina

Mucosa

Continuous with the uterus.

  • Tissue: non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium and areolar CT. Creates transverse folds called rugae.
  • Antigen-presenting (dendritic) cells found here participate in transmission of viruses (HIV).
  • Large stores of glycogen, which can be decomposed into organic acids.
  • Acidic environment inhibits microbial growth. This is protective, but the same acidity is hostile to sperm. (Hence the alkaline buffering provided by semen.)

Muscularis

  • Composed of an outer circular layer and an inner longitudinal layer of smooth muscle.
  • Function: stretches to accommodate the penis during intercourse and a baby during childbirth.

Adventitia

  • Superficial layer.
  • Made of areolar CT.
  • Anchors the vagina to adjacent organs: urethra and urinary bladder anteriorly, rectum posteriorly.

Hymen

Thin fold of vascularized mucous membrane that forms a border or partially closes the inferior end of the vaginal opening (vaginal orifice).

Ruptures following first sexual intercourse.

Clinical Correlation: Imperforate Hymen

When the hymen completely covers the orifice and blocks the discharge of menstrual flow. May need to be surgically opened.

External Genitalia, Perineum & Mammary Glands

Many structures of the vulva are homologous to male structures. Same embryonic origin, different final form. Knowing the homologies makes the anatomy easier to remember.

External Genitalia

Components of the Vulva (Pudendum)

1. Mons Pubis

Elevation of adipose tissue covered by skin and coarse pubic hair. Function: cushions the pubic symphysis.

2. Labia Majora

Two large longitudinal folds of skin covered by pubic hair. Contain adipose tissue, sebaceous (oil) glands, and apocrine (sweat) glands.

Homologous to the scrotum.

3. Labia Minora

Smaller folds. No hair, no fat. Few sudoriferous glands but many sebaceous glands.

Homologous to the spongy urethra.

4. Clitoris

Small cylindrical mass containing two erectile bodies (corpora cavernosa), numerous nerves, and blood vessels.

  • Location: anterior junction of the labia minora.
  • Prepuce of the clitoris: point where the labia minora unite and cover the clitoris with skin.
  • Glans clitoris: the exposed part.
  • Function: responds to tactile stimulation. Plays a role in sexual excitement.

Homologous to the penis.

5. Vestibule

Region between the labia minora.

Contains: hymen, vaginal orifice, external urethral orifice, and several duct/gland openings.

Homologous to the male intermediate (membranous) urethra.

More Vulva Structures

Orifices, Glands & Erectile Tissue

Vaginal Orifice

Opening of the vagina to the exterior.

External Urethral Orifice

Opening of the urethra to the exterior.

Paraurethral Glands (Skene's Glands)

On either side of the external urethral orifice. Secrete mucus. Embedded in the wall of the urethra.

Homologous to the prostate.

Greater Vestibular Glands (Bartholin's Glands)

On either side of the vaginal orifice. Produce a small amount of mucus during sexual arousal to aid lubrication.

Homologous to the bulbourethral (Cowper's) glands in males.

Bulb of the Vestibule

Two elongated masses of erectile tissue deep to the vaginal orifice. Becomes engorged with blood during sexual arousal. Narrows and puts pressure on the penis during intercourse.

Homologous to the corpus spongiosum and bulb of the penis in males.

Regional Anatomy

Perineum

Definition: The diamond-shaped area medial to the thighs and buttocks, in both males and females.

Contents

External genitals and anus.

Boundaries

  • Anteriorly: pubic symphysis
  • Laterally: ischial tuberosities
  • Posteriorly: coccyx

Two Triangles

Draw a transverse line between the ischial tuberosities:

  • Urogenital triangle (anterior): contains the external genitalia.
  • Anal triangle (posterior): contains the anus.

Lactation Apparatus

Mammary Glands: Structure

Project anteriorly from two muscles: pectoralis major and serratus anterior. A layer of fascia (dense irregular CT) attaches the breast to these muscles.

Specialized Structures

  1. Nipple. Pigmented projection containing a series of openings of lactiferous ducts where milk emerges.
    • Areola: circular pigmented area surrounding the nipple. Contains modified sebaceous (oil) glands.
  2. Suspensory ligaments of the breast (Cooper's ligaments). Between skin and fascia. Support the breast. Become looser with age or excessive strain (jogging, high impact aerobics, sports).
  3. Mammary gland. A modified sudoriferous (sweat) gland that produces milk.
  4. Lobes: 25 to 50 lobes or compartments. Lobes divide into lobules containing alveoli (milk-producing structures).
  5. Lactiferous ducts. Drain milk from the alveoli and transport it to the nipple.
  6. Myoepithelial cells. Contracting cells that propel milk toward the nipple.

Function: Lactation

Milk production and ejection. Stimulus: baby suckling on the nipple stimulates the release of oxytocin from the posterior pituitary, which triggers milk ejection.

Blood Supply

Internal thoracic artery, lateral thoracic artery, and intercostal arteries.
