📖 Welcome -- Read This First

This is your clickable Module 5 study tool. Four content tabs (Urinary, Reproductive + Pelvis, Spinal Cord + PNS, Brain + CSF) mirror your structure list handout, with each structure as a checkable item.

Skim this tab once, then spend your study time in the content tabs.

📌How to Use This Tool

  • Tap a region header to expand or collapse that section.
  • Click any structure to check it off. The progress bar at the top tracks the active tab.
  • Gold-bordered Q cards are conceptual questions from the handout. Try to answer from memory before peeking at the note below.
  • Path tracings (blood, urine, sperm, CSF, Circle of Willis) appear in framed "one-liner" boxes at the end of their sections -- these are the exact strings you want to have memorized.
  • Expand the Abbreviations Key bar at the top of any content tab if you need a quick reminder of what CN, DRG, ACA, PICA, etc. stand for.

🎯Study Strategy That Actually Works

Active retrieval beats re-reading. Reading the structure list while nodding along is NOT studying. Retrieving it from memory -- saying it, pointing to it, spelling it -- IS studying.

Only check an item off after you have retrieved the answer from your brain without looking, not after you have read it. The checkbox is a self-honesty tool.

Separate what you know from what you don't

Space your retrieval over multiple days rather than cramming. Items you miss get more reps; items you know stay parked until closer to the exam. That is the whole trick.

Practice modalities -- use several
  • Lab Testing Book: Unlabeled images in page protectors + thin dry-erase markers. Label, check, wipe, repeat.
  • Digital practice: PowerPoint of blank + answer-key images, exported to PDF, loaded into GoodNotes on an iPad with Apple Pencil.
  • Cadaver, models, microscope slides: Still the gold standard -- this tool does not replace lab time.

👥Round Table Format (For Lab Table Study Groups)

Use this during class at your lab table or in study groups. Every student participates -- no opting out. Mistakes are expected and required.

  1. ID out loud. One person identifies all structures out loud for the group. Start with 5 to 8 structures, then add more each round until you hit 15 to 20.
  2. Read + point. One person reads structures off the list randomly; the student being tested points to each. Rotate so everyone reads and everyone is tested.
  3. Reverse it -- and spell. Tester points to a structure. The student being tested says it aloud AND spells it if spelling is shaky.
  4. Full run. Each person takes a turn pointing to every structure on the model in one continuous pass, naming each from memory.
  5. Add context. Now add functions, composition, and distinguishing characteristics as you name each structure. Example: "Point to and name the structure that passes through the optic canal." This connects lecture to lab so it stops feeling like two separate courses.

You may be tested on this in class. Be prepared, not stressed. Diligent work on meaningful tasks is what makes the grade.

⛔What This Tool Is NOT

  • Not a replacement for hands-on cadaver, model, microscope, or imaging practice.
  • Not a substitute for active retrieval with classmates.
  • Not a lecture reference -- this is the lab structure list, keyed to the lab portion of the exam.

Bottom line: Active learning feels slower than re-reading notes. It is. It also works -- the feeling of slowness is the feeling of building durable memory. Retrieve, mess up, learn, retrieve again. That is the job.

  • ✓

Spinal Cord Supply
  • ✓
    Anterior spinal artery (formed by branches from both vertebrals)
Circle of Willis closure
Anterior circulation (ICA → ACA / MCA) + posterior circulation (vertebrals → basilar → PCA) join via the anterior communicating a. (front) and the paired posterior communicating a. (sides) to form a complete ring at the base of the brain.
